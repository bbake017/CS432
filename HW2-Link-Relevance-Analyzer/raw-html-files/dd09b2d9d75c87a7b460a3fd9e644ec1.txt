<p>Donald Trump's <a href="http://www.npr.org/2016/08/09/489364948/trump-appears-to-suggest-second-amendment-could-stop-clinton">suggestion</a> that "maybe there is" something Second Amendment supporters could do to stop his Democratic rival from picking Supreme Court justices caused outrage among those who read it as an incitement to violence.</p> <p>Trump says he was merely talking about the political power of gun-rights advocates and the National Rifle Association to defeat Hillary Clinton. But it's far from the first time the Republican nominee has made a statement that seems open to sinister interpretations.</p> <p><a href="http://www.npr.org/2016/03/29/472232836/donald-trump-s-word-choices-parsed-by-fans-and-critics">As we've noted before</a>, Trump has repeatedly made public comments that have seemed to many observers to push or violate the boundaries of acceptable political discourse in the United States.</p> <aside aria-label="advertisement" class="ad-wrap backstage" id="ad-backstage-wrap">

<p><strong>Russian email hackers</strong></p> <p>Just <a href="http://www.npr.org/2016/07/27/487665614/trump-urges-russia-to-find-clintons-emails-and-release-them">a couple of weeks ago</a>, the real estate developer held a press conference in Florida where he suggested that Russian hackers should look for emails missing from Hillary Clinton's time as secretary of state.</p> <p>"Russia, if you're listening, I hope you're able to find the 30,000 emails that are missing," Trump said.</p> <p>Pressed by reporters on whether he was concerned about the idea of Russians looking at private State Department emails, Trump said he was not. He did not clarify his remarks until later, after the Clinton campaign accused Trump of encouraging Russian hacking.</p> <p>Initially, Trump's spokesman, Jason Miller, tweeted: "To be clear, Mr. Trump did not call on or invite Russia or anyone else to hack Hillary Clinton's emails today."</p> <p>But that interpretation differed even from <a href="http://www.cnn.com/2016/07/28/politics/donald-trump-russia-hacking-sarcastic/">an explanation offered later</a> by Trump himself, who told Fox News the next day that "of course I'm being sarcastic."</p> <p><strong>Wanting to "hit" his opponents</strong></p> <p>Trump got into trouble for seeming to encourage violent behavior at his rallies after several violent clashes between protesters and supporters during the primary season. Trump suggested he would <a href="http://www.latimes.com/politics/la-na-trump-campaign-protests-20160313-story.html">pay the legal fees</a> of a man accused of punching a protester at a rally in North Carolina in March. He <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=S3HUVIZQc-Q">also urged</a> supporters to "knock the crap out" of anyone trying to disrupt his rally by throwing tomatoes. He later backed off those statements, <a href="http://www.cbsnews.com/news/donald-trump-i-dont-condone-violence/">telling CBS News</a>, "I don't condone violence."</p> <aside aria-label="advertisement" class="ad-wrap secondary" id="ad-secondary-wrap">

<p>More recently, while campaigning during the Democratic National Convention, Trump repeatedly told supporters he'd like to "hit" his critics who were attacking him from the convention stage  including former New York Mayor Michael Bloomberg. <a href="http://www.cnn.com/2016/07/28/politics/donald-trump-dnc-response/">CNN ran that headline</a>, while also noting that Trump often uses that term as a rhetorical device to describe verbal attacks.</p> <p>Even so, <a href="https://www.c-span.org/video/?413365-1/donald-trump-campaigns-denver-colorado">during a rally</a> at the Wings Over the Rockies Air &amp; Space Museum in Denver, Trump doubled down, promising to "hit them back so hard." At that rally, Trump made clear that he'd "do it verbally" and on social media, and also attacked the media for questioning the statement.</p> <p>"CNN put on, 'Trump is going to hit them.' I mean, I'm gonna hit them with this, with the lips, I'm gonna hit them verbally," Trump said, motioning toward his mouth. "They put out a thing, 'Trump hitting  .' You never saw people  these people are so dishonest. They're so dishonest. But I'm gonna hit 'em hard."</p> <p><strong>Double entendre and innuendo</strong></p> <p>Many of Trump's opaque statements seem to rely on suggestion and innuendo. It's been a year since Trump, displeased with Fox News host Megyn Kelly's tough questions at the first Republican presidential debate, <a href="http://www.npr.org/2015/08/08/430763868/following-controversial-remarks-trump-gets-uninvited-to-conservative-confab">told CNN</a> that Kelly had "blood coming out of her eyes, blood coming out of her wherever." That comment was widely viewed as referring to Kelly's menstrual cycle  though Trump claimed his comments were misinterpreted. "Only a deviant would think that," Trump said.</p> <p>But there have been other remarks that appear to fall along similar lines. Trump mocked 2012 GOP nominee Mitt Romney after <a href="http://www.npr.org/2016/03/03/469083102/former-gop-nominee-mitt-romney-sharply-criticizes-donald-trump">Romney criticized him</a>, saying the former Massachusetts governor would have "dropped to his knees" for Trump's endorsement four years ago. Also in March, during the primary season, there was <a href="http://www.nbcnewyork.com/news/national-international/Donald-Trump-Hand-Size-Theres-No-Problem-371009841.html">the infamous "hand size" discussion</a>, when then-rival Florida Sen. Marco Rubio raised questions about Trump's <a href="http://www.nbcnews.com/politics/2016-election/donald-trump-has-small-hands-marco-rubio-says-n527791">ostensibly small hands</a> (a concern that <a href="http://www.npr.org/2016/03/07/469209254/decades-later-spy-magazine-founders-continue-to-torment-trump">has plagued Trump</a> since the 1980s). Trump <a href="http://www.npr.org/2016/06/17/482534352/tiny-superpac-trolls-donald-trump-about-his-tiny-hands">objected</a>, saying, "Look at those hands, are they small hands? And, he referred to my hands  'If they're small, something else must be small.' I guarantee you there's no problem. I guarantee."</p> <aside aria-label="advertisement" class="ad-wrap third" id="ad-third-wrap">

<p><strong>"Dishonest media"</strong></p> <p>The response to these vague statements from Trump tends to follow a pattern:</p> <p>1. Trump makes an apparently offensive remark.</p> <p>2. Criticism ensues from Democrats.</p> <p>3. The media go into a frenzy, with several news cycles of coverage.</p> <p>4. Trump and his allies complain that his words have been distorted, taken out of context or overblown.</p> <p>Over the past year or more, this cycle has played out like a ritual again and again  keeping Trump in the spotlight and feeding news outlets' appetite for headlines. It's also fed Trump's narrative that the media are a mouthpiece for the Clinton campaign.</p> <p>After Trump's remarks about the Second Amendment this week, his campaign released a statement titled "Trump Campaign Statement on Dishonest Media." Spokesman Jason Miller said Trump's remarks were about the "amazing spirit" of Second Amendment advocates, "which gives them great political power."</p> <div class="bucketwrap internallink insettwocolumn inset2col" id="res489490225">

<p>That approach worked well for Trump in the primaries, when he excited many core GOP voters with his bluntness and plainspoken, visceral style. Many Republican-leaning voters have told reporters they liked Trump's directness, even if they sometimes wished he'd moderate his tone. Many have also said they don't take his more outrageous statements literally, but admire that he's not afraid to say them.</p> <p>But it's less clear that this style will work for Trump with general election voters. Since the Democratic convention, he's been <a href="http://www.npr.org/2016/08/06/488969831/as-trump-poll-numbers-slip-he-warns-of-a-rigged-election">slipping in polls</a> of likely voters in <a href="http://www.realclearpolitics.com/epolls/2016/president/us/general_election_trump_vs_clinton_vs_johnson_vs_stein-5952.html">key battleground states</a>. About <a href="http://www.vox.com/2016/8/9/12404942/poll-donald-trump">two-thirds of voters</a> in a recent <em>Washington Post</em>-ABC News poll said they didn't believe Trump has the temperament to be president  a concern that may be exacerbated by Trump's pattern of making questionable statements, and a charge Hillary Clinton has picked up on.</p>
