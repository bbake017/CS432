<p>NEW YORK — A federal jury has found former President Donald Trump liable for battery and defamation in the lawsuit brought by writer E. Jean Carroll, who says he raped her in a Manhattan department store in the mid-1990s. </p> <p>The nine jurors, who deliberated for barely three hours before reaching their unanimous conclusion, did not find that Trump raped Carroll. But they agreed that he "sexually abused" her and that he defamed her when he denied her story. </p> <p>Carroll was awarded $5 million in total damages for both claims. </p> <aside aria-label="advertisement" class="ad-wrap backstage" id="ad-backstage-wrap">

<p>"Today, the world <em>finally </em>knows the truth. This victory is not just for me but for every woman who has suffered because she was not believed," Carroll said in a statement. </p> <p>In an email to NPR, a lawyer representing Trump said the former president would appeal the decision. </p> <p>Over the course of two weeks in a federal courtroom in New York City, jurors heard Carroll's story of a flirty-turned-violent encounter with Trump at the Bergdorf Goodman department store in the mid-1990s, at a time when her career as a writer and advice columnist was at its peak. </p> <p>Carroll testified the incident left her unable to form romantic relationships and that her career suffered after she made her allegation public. </p> <div class="bucketwrap internallink insettwocolumn inset2col" id="res1175001746">

<!-- END ID="RES1175001746" CLASS="BUCKETWRAP INTERNALLINK INSETTWOCOLUMN INSET2COL " -->
<p>In 2019, she sued Trump for defamation over his denial of her claim. That lawsuit has been hung up in federal court over the question of whether Trump could be sued for a statement he made while president.</p> <p><a href="https://www.npr.org/2023/04/09/1168664355/trump-e-jean-carroll-lawsuit-trial">She filed a second lawsuit in 2022</a>, this time adding a battery claim, after the state of New York temporarily removed the statute of limitations for sexual assault survivors. </p> <p>Trump did not appear in court. He has consistently denied Carroll's claims and continued to do so Tuesday after the jury's decision. "I HAVE ABSOLUTELY NO IDEA WHO THIS WOMAN IS. THIS VERDICT IS A DISGRACE - A CONTINUATION OF THE GREATEST WITCH HUNT OF ALL TIME!" he <a href="https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/110340379870475514">wrote on Truth Social</a>. <p>After the verdict was read, Carroll left the courthouse accompanied by<strong> </strong>her lawyer Roberta Kaplan, who said, "We're very happy." Carroll did not speak to reporters, but she smiled as she departed.</p> <p>As a civil trial, the burden of proof for the battery claim was lower than in a criminal proceeding. Rather than be certain "beyond a reasonable doubt," as criminal trials require, Carroll needed to prove her case "by a preponderance of the evidence" — in other words, the jurors needed only to believe Carroll's version of events was more likely true than not. </p> <div class="bucketwrap internallink insettwocolumn inset2col" id="res1175002564">

<p>In New York, a civil claim of battery may encompass a wide range of unwanted physical contact. In addition to asking whether Trump "raped" Carroll, the jury was asked to consider whether he "sexually abused" her or "forcibly touched" her. </p> <p>"Anything from a gentle but unwanted peck on the cheek to stabbing somebody with a knife could be battery for purposes of a civil case like this one," U.S. District Judge Lewis A. Kaplan told jurors at the start of the trial. </p> <p>In finding Trump liable for battery, the jury awarded Carroll $2 million in compensatory damages, along with $20,000 in punitive damages. For the defamation claim, the jury awarded her $2.7 million in compensatory damages and an additional $280,000 for punitive damages, finding that Trump had acted "maliciously, out of hatred, ill will, spite or wanton, reckless, or willful disregard of the rights of another" <a href="https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/109158644496040450">when he accused her of inventing the story</a>. </p> <p>Over three days on the witness stand, <a href="https://www.npr.org/2023/04/26/1172189592/e-jean-carroll-rape-lawsuit-trial-trump">Carroll described the alleged incident in detail</a>. Their meeting was a chance encounter, she said — the two recognized each other, and Trump invited her to help him shop for a gift for another woman. </p> <p>At first, she felt "delighted" and "enchanted" to be shopping with Trump, Carroll testified. The two flirted.</p> <div class="bucketwrap internallink insettwocolumn inset2col" id="res1175002919">

And his defense team called no witnesses. </p> <p>Instead, his lawyers worked to sow doubt in Carroll's story. </p> <p>Led by defense attorney Joe Tacopina, they targeted Carroll's inability to recall the precise date — or even the year — of the alleged encounter.
