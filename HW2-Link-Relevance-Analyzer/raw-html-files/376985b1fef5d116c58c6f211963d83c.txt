<p><strong>Updated at 4:40 a.m. ET Wednesday</strong></p> <p>Since early May, 2,342 children have been separated from their parents after crossing the Southern U.S. border, according to the Department of Homeland Security, as part of a new immigration strategy by the Trump administration that has prompted widespread outcry.</p> <p>On Wednesday, President Trump signed an executive order reversing his policy of separating families — and replacing it with a policy of detaining entire families together, including children, but ignoring legal time limits on the detention of minors.</p> <p>Here's what we know about the family separation policy, its history and its effects:</p> <aside aria-label="advertisement" class="ad-wrap backstage" id="ad-backstage-wrap">

<h3 class="edTag">Did the Trump administration have a policy of separating families at the border?</h3> <p>Yes.</p> <p>In April, U.S. Attorney General Jeff Sessions ordered prosecutors along the border to "adopt immediately a zero-tolerance policy" for illegal border crossings. That included prosecuting parents traveling with their children as well as people who subsequently attempted to request asylum.</p> <div class="bucketwrap listtext" id="res621353109">
<div class="bucket">
<p><strong>In Their Own Words</strong></p> <p><a href="https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2018/jun/18/donald-trump-us-migrant-camp-border-separation">President Trump</a>: "The United States will not be a migrant camp and it will not be a refugee holding facility. ... Not on my watch."</p> <p><a data-key="443928" href="https://www.npr.org/sections/thetwo-way/2018/05/07/609225537/sessions-says-zero-tolerance-for-illegal-border-crossers-vows-to-divide-families">Attorney General Jeff Sessions</a>: "If you cross this border unlawfully, then we will prosecute you. It's that simple. ... If you are smuggling a child, then we will prosecute you and that child will be separated from you as required by law. If you don't like that, then don't smuggle children over our border."</p> <p><a href="http://www.foxnews.com/transcript/2018/06/18/sessions-defends-zero-tolerance-immigration-policy.html">Sessions</a> on whether the policy is a deterrent: "Yes, hopefully people will get the message and come through the border at the port of entry and not break across the border unlawfully."</p> <p><a data-key="460205" href="https://www.npr.org/2018/05/10/610113364/transcript-homeland-security-secretary-kirstjen-nielsens-full-interview-with-npr">Homeland Security Secretary Kirstjen Nielsen</a>: Under the "zero tolerance" policy, when families cross the border illegally, "Operationally, what that means is we will have to separate your family. That's no different than what we do every day in every part of the United States when an adult of a family commits a crime."</p> <p><a href="https://www.npr.org/2018/05/11/610116389/transcript-white-house-chief-of-staff-john-kellys-interview-with-npr">White House chief of staff John Kelly</a>: Separating families is "a tough deterrent. ... The children will be taken care of — put into foster care or whatever. But the big point is they elected to come illegally into the United States and this is a technique that no one hopes will be used extensively or for very long."</p>
</div>
<!-- END CLASS="BUCKET" -->
</div>
<!-- END ID="RES621353109" CLASS="BUCKETWRAP LISTTEXT" -->
<p>White House officials have repeatedly acknowledged that under that policy, they separate all families who cross the border. Sessions has described it as deterrence.</p> <p>U.S. Customs and Border Protection explains <a href="https://www.cbp.gov/newsroom/zero-tolerance-immigration-prosecutions-family-fact-sheet">on its site</a> and <a href="https://www.cbp.gov/sites/default/files/assets/documents/2018-Jun/next-steps-for-families.pdf">in a flyer</a> that border-crossing families will be separated.</p> <p>The policy was unique to the Trump administration. Previous administrations did not, as a general principle, separate all families crossing the U.S. border illegally.</p> <h3 class="edTag">What policy did Trump enact on Wednesday?</h3> <p>On Wednesday, Trump ended the policy of family separation and replaced it with a policy of family detention.</p> <p>He signed an executive order that kept the zero-tolerance policy in place — but added, "It is also the policy of this Administration to maintain family unity, including by detaining alien families together where appropriate and consistent with law and available resources." It did provide an exception for when authorities believe keeping the family together would be harmful for the child.</p> <aside aria-label="advertisement" class="ad-wrap secondary" id="ad-secondary-wrap">
<div class="ad-header"><span>Sponsor Message</span></div>

<p>In signing the order, Trump noted "there may be some litigation" — that is, a legal challenge to the new policy.</p> <p>A 2015 court order, based on a document called the Flores settlement, prevents the government from keeping migrant children in detention for more than 20 days. Trump has instructed Attorney General Jeff Sessions to ask the federal court to modify that agreement in order to allow children, and by extension, unified families, to be kept in detention without time limit.</p> <p>The request asks, specifically, for permission from the courts "to detain alien families together throughout the pendency of criminal proceedings for improper entry or any removal or other immigration proceedings."</p> <p>Trump also calls for branches of his administration to make facilities available for detaining families with children — and calls on the Defense Department, to build new facilities "if necessary."</p> <p>The Obama administration practiced family detention, until the court order prohibited it. Many of the same groups that have vocally denounced family separation are also<em> </em>opposed to family detention, and had urged supervised release instead.</p> <p>Children currently remain separated from their parents. In signing the order, Trump said it would keep families together "in the immediate days forward." It is not clear when or how currently separated families will be reunited.</p> <h3 class="edTag">What happens when families are separated?</h3> <p>The process begins at a Customs and Border Protection detention facility. But many details about what happens next — how children are taken from their parents and by whom — were unclear.</p> <p>According to the Texas Civil Rights Project, which has been able to speak with detained adults, multiple parents reported that they were separated from their children <a href="https://texascivilrightsproject.org/civil-rights-groups-family-separations/">and not given any information about where their children would go</a>. The organization also says that in some cases, the children were taken away under the pretense that they would be getting a bath.</p> <aside aria-label="advertisement" class="ad-wrap third" id="ad-third-wrap">

<h3 class="edTag"><strong>Where have children gone once they've been separated? </strong></h3> <p>The answer varies over time. Children begin at Customs and Border Protection facilities, are transferred to longer-term shelters and are supposed to eventually be placed with families or sponsors. Here's more about each step:</p> <p><strong>Customs and Border Protection facilities.</strong><em> </em>If you've seen photos of children in what look like chain-link cages — whether <a href="https://www.azcentral.com/story/news/politics/immigration/2014/06/18/arizona-immigrant-children-holding-area-tour/10780449/">unaccompanied minors in 2014</a> or <a href="https://www.cnn.com/2018/06/18/politics/immigration-mcallen-border-patrol-photos/index.html">separated children in 2018</a> — they are probably photos from a Customs and Border Protection facility.</p> <p>Children usually are held here initially, but it is illegal to keep them for more than three days — these holding cells are not meant for long-term detention.</p> <div class="bucketwrap internallink insettwocolumn inset2col" id="res621172173">

<p>For 15 years, ORR has handled the "care and placement" of unaccompanied migrant children. Until recently, that usually meant minors who crossed into the U.S. alone. Now it also includes children who have been separated from their families by authorities, including much younger children.</p> <p>On a call with reporters on Tuesday, a Border Patrol official said that it's a matter of "discretion" how young is too young for a child to be separated from their parents. In general, he said, the age of 5 has been used as a benchmark, with children younger than that called "tender-aged."</p> <p>The CEO of Southwest Key, which operates 26 ORR shelters, tells NPR the children at his facilities range from ages "zero to 17."</p> <p>On the same call, an HHS official said that some of the ORR shelters are specifically equipped to take care of children younger than 13. He provided few details and could not say how many children under 13, under 5 or under 2 are currently being held by HHS.</p> <p>Now <a href="https://apnews.com/dc0c9a5134d14862ba7c7ad9a811160e">The Associated Press reports</a> that it has located three centers in Texas that "have been rapidly repurposed to serve needs of children including some under 5," with a fourth center scheduled to open in Houston. Infants are among the detained children, the AP reports.</p> <div class="bucketwrap internallink insettwocolumn inset2col" id="res621171768">

<p>ORR has a network of about 100 shelter facilities, all operated by nonprofit groups, where children are detained.</p> <p>NPR's John Burnett recently joined other reporters to visit one such facility, a converted Walmart Supercenter housing nearly 1,500 boys ages 10 to 17. Journalists' access to that facility in Brownsville, Texas, was limited, but the site was markedly different from CBP facilities seen in photos released by the government — the teenage boys slept on beds instead of mats on the floor, in rooms instead of cages, and had access to classes and games.</p> <p>ORR <a href="https://www.acf.hhs.gov/orr/unaccompanied-children-frequently-asked-questions">says</a> children remain at these shelters for "fewer than 57 days on average." However some children have been kept detained for <a href="https://www.npr.org/2018/06/11/618831715/more-than-10-000-migrant-children-are-in-u-s-government-custody">months longer than that</a>, and some advocates say certain facilities improperly administer psychotropic medications.</p> <aside aria-label="advertisement" class="ad-wrap overflow-4" id="ad-overflow-4-wrap">

<p>Observers have raised concerns about the psychological toll on young children who enter this shelter system. NPR's Joel Rose <a href="https://www.npr.org/2018/06/15/620254326/doctors-warn-about-dangers-of-child-separations">talked to one former shelter employee</a> who said he quit after he was instructed to prevent siblings from hugging each other. The organization that runs the shelter said it allows touching and hugging in certain circumstances.</p> <div class="bucketwrap listtext" id="res621349778">
<div class="bucket">
<p><strong>Where Are The Girls And Young Children? <br/></strong>Official photos and videos have shown only older boys at shelter facilities. <br/><br/>The Department of Health and Human Services says there are specialized shelters for children under 13. No images from those shelters have been released, but authorities say new images and videos will be provided later this week.</p> <p>The Associated Press says it has identified three shelters in Texas that are housing young children, including infants. The locations of those shelters were not released by the government.</p>

<p>More than 10,000 migrant children, including children who crossed the border alone, are kept in ORR facilities. And existing facilities are filling up — the shelter Burnett visited was 95 percent full.</p> <p><strong>Tent camps</strong><strong><em>.</em></strong><em> </em>A temporary facility has been set up in Tornillo, Texas, near El Paso. Little is known about the facility, and reporters have not been allowed inside, but KQED's John Sepulvado has seen the tent camp from outside.</p> <p>"It's a heavy-duty-grade white tent in the middle of a desert," he <a href="http://www.wbur.org/hereandnow/2018/06/18/border-immigration-families-unaccompanied-minors">told NPR's Here &amp; Now</a>. "It's behind two chain-link fences and there's a dirt easement that's on top of it, so you can't actually see into it from the American side."</p> <div class="bucketwrap image large" id="res621176934">

His advisers have presented a more complicated argument for how the law requires family separation.</p> <p>"The laws prohibit us from detaining families while they go through prosecution," Nielsen said on Monday — a reference to the 20-day limits on how long children can be detained. Therefore, she says, "we cannot detain families together."</p> <p>She argues that that leaves the administration with the options of not enforcing the law, which it rejects, or separating families. But immigration advocates and legal experts say that there are other options, including those that previous administrations have chosen.</p> <p>Trump's new order has effectively requested a change to the existing law, to loosen restrictions on the detention of children.</p> <h3 class="edTag"><strong>What was the policy under President Obama?</strong></h3> <p>The Obama administration <a href="https://www.npr.org/sections/thetwo-way/2014/06/20/323980057/u-s-will-open-immigrant-family-detention-centers-target-rumors-of-laxity">established family detention centers</a> that kept families together while their cases were processed. Trump's executive order appears to effectively revive this policy.</p> <aside aria-label="advertisement" class="ad-wrap overflow-7" id="ad-overflow-7-wrap">
<div class="ad-header"><span>Sponsor Message</span></div>
<div class="ad-config-wrap">
<div class="ad-config ad-overflow-7 has-refresh-enabled" data-ad-config='{"network":"\/6735\/","zone":"News_National","targets":{"testserver":"false","isPodcastEpisode":"false","storyId":"621065383","agg":["434975886","593868387"]},"site":{"desktop":"NPROverflow","mobile":"NPRMobileOverflow"},"location":"overflow-7","deferred":false,"isBetweenContent":true,"hasRefreshEnabled":true,"isOutstreamVideoEnabled":false}' id="ad-overflow-7-News_National">
<div style="min-height:90px"></div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="ad-footer"></div>
</aside>
<p>The Obama-era centers were <a href="http://www.usccb.org/about/migration-policy/position-papers/upload/family-detention.pdf">sharply criticized</a> for keeping children detained even if they were still with their parents. A court ruled that those detention centers <a href="https://www.npr.org/2015/07/27/426674309/after-court-ruling-3-immigration-detention-centers-could-close">violated the Flores agreement</a> and that families should be released together.</p> <p>The Obama White House also<strong> </strong>had a policy of releasing families through a program called Alternatives to Detention that still allowed them to be closely supervised — for instance, by giving mothers ankle monitors before releasing them.</p> <div class="bucketwrap internallink insettwocolumn inset2col" id="res621172423">
<div class="bucket img">
<div class="bucketblock">
<h3 class="slug"><a href="https://www.npr.org/sections/thetwo-way/">The Two-Way </a></h3>
<h3><a data-metrics-ga4='{"category":"recirculation","action":"story_recirculation_click","clickType":"inset box","clickUrl":"https:\/\/www.npr.org\/sections\/thetwo-way\/2015\/06\/24\/417204277\/obama-administration-acts-to-ease-family-detentions"}' href="https://www.npr.org/sections/thetwo-way/2015/06/24/417204277/obama-administration-acts-to-ease-family-detentions">Obama Administration Acts To Ease Family Detentions</a></h3>
</div>
<!-- END CLASS="BUCKETBLOCK" -->
</div>
<!-- END CLASS="BUCKET IMG" -->
</div>
<!-- END ID="RES621172423" CLASS="BUCKETWRAP INTERNALLINK INSETTWOCOLUMN INSET2COL " -->
<p>The ACLU <a href="https://www.aclu.org/other/aclu-fact-sheet-alternatives-immigration-detention-atd">welcomed the Alternatives to Detention program</a>, but <a href="http://immigrationimpact.com/2012/07/11/for-immigrants-alternatives-to-detention-not-all-theyre-cracked-up-to-be/">other immigrant-rights groups had reservations</a>.</p> <p>As Burnett reported, one for-profit prison company that was making money off immigrant detention <a href="https://www.npr.org/2015/11/13/455790454/as-asylum-seekers-swap-prison-beds-for-ankle-bracelets-same-firm-profits">was also profiting off those ankle monitor systems</a>.</p> <p>ICE tells NPR that the Alternatives to Detention program is still active under the Trump administration, but Trump has repeatedly said he opposes what he denounces as "catch and release."</p> <h3 class="edTag"><strong>Can families request asylum, allowing them to stay together?</strong></h3> <div class="bucketwrap listtext" id="res621387045">
<div class="bucket">
<p><strong>What Is Asylum?</strong></p> <p>Seeking asylum means asking the U.S. to accept you — legally — because of persecution you are facing in your home country.</p> <p>Crossing the border illegally is a misdemeanor; for a person who has already been deported once, it's a felony. Both types of crimes are currently being prosecuted with no exceptions, even if a person later requests asylum.</p> <p>Seeking asylum<em> at a port of entry</em>, however, is not a crime at all.</p>
</div>
<!-- END CLASS="BUCKET" -->
</div>
<!-- END ID="RES621387045" CLASS="BUCKETWRAP LISTTEXT" -->
<p>Hypothetically, yes. In practice, maybe not.</p> <p>Families that request asylum at ports of entry are meant to be kept together while their claims are processed.</p> <p>But there is evidence that even families who seek asylum at ports of entry are being separated. One high-profile case involves a Congolese woman who sought asylum and still was separated from her 7-year-old daughter. In February, NPR's Burnett <a href="https://www.npr.org/2018/02/27/589079243/activists-outraged-that-u-s-border-agents-separate-immigrant-families">reported</a> on the <a href="https://www.aclu.org/sites/default/files/field_document/71_mtd_order.pdf">legal battle</a> of <em>Ms. L v. ICE</em>.</p> <div class="bucketwrap internallink insettwocolumn inset2col" id="res621090156">
<div class="bucket img">

<h3 class="slug"><a href="https://www.npr.org/series/690257428/asylum-crackdown">National </a></h3>
<h3><a data-metrics-ga4='{"category":"recirculation","action":"story_recirculation_click","clickType":"inset box","clickUrl":"https:\/\/www.npr.org\/2018\/06\/15\/620310589\/after-a-2-000-mile-asylum-journey-family-is-turned-away-before-reaching-u-s-soil"}' href="https://www.npr.org/2018/06/15/620310589/after-a-2-000-mile-asylum-journey-family-is-turned-away-before-reaching-u-s-soil">After Traveling 2,000 Miles For Asylum, This Family's Journey Halts At A Bridge</a></h3>
</div>
<!-- END CLASS="BUCKETBLOCK" -->
</div>
<!-- END CLASS="BUCKET IMG" -->
</div>
<!-- END ID="RES621090156" CLASS="BUCKETWRAP INTERNALLINK INSETTWOCOLUMN INSET2COL " -->
<p>Hers is not an isolated case, according to immigrant advocates.</p> <p>"Lutheran Immigration and Refugee Service has documented 53 incidents of family separation in the last nine months, mostly Central Americans. Other immigrant support groups say there are many more cases," Burnett reported.</p> <p>Reporter Jean Guerrero of KPBS in San Diego <a href="https://www.pbs.org/newshour/show/migrant-seeking-asylum-says-his-toddler-was-taken-away-at-the-u-s-border">reported on the case of a Salvadoran father</a>, Jose Demar Fuentes, who says he sought asylum and was separated from his 1-year-old son, Mateo, despite having an original birth certificate proving that he is the boy's father.</p> <div aria-label="Tweet" class="bucketwrap twitter medium graphic" id="res1008467510906228736">
<blockquote class="twitter-tweet"><p dir="ltr" lang="en">For those seeking asylum at ports of entry, we have continued the policy from previous Administrations and will only separate if the child is in danger, there is no custodial relationship between 'family' members, or if the adult has broken a law.</p>— Secretary Kirstjen M. Nielsen (@SecNielsen) <a href="https://twitter.com/SecNielsen/status/1008467510906228736?ref_src=twsrc%5Etfw">June 17, 2018</a></blockquote>
<script async="" charset="utf-8" src="https://platform.twitter.com/widgets.js"></script>
</div>
<!-- END ID="RES1008467510906228736" CLASS="BUCKETWRAP TWITTER MEDIUM GRAPHIC" ARIA-LABEL="TWEET" -->
<p>In a White House press briefing Monday, Nielsen said, "DHS is not separating families legitimately seeking asylum at ports of entry." But she said DHS "will only separate a family if we cannot determine there is a familial relationship, if child is at risk with the parent or legal guardian, or if the parent or legal guardian is referred for prosecution."</p> <p>Burnett also <a href="https://www.npr.org/2018/06/15/620310589/after-a-2-000-mile-asylum-journey-family-is-turned-away-before-reaching-u-s-soil">has reported</a> that some families are not being allowed to request asylum — that they are being repeatedly turned away and told the CBP facility is too full to accept them.</p> <p>Nielsen has denied that some asylum-seekers who present themselves at a port of entry are <a href="https://www.texasmonthly.com/politics/immigrant-advocates-question-legality-of-latest-federal-tactics/">being turned away,</a> which would be a violation of international law.</p> <p>"We are saying we want to take care of you in the right way. Right now we do not have the resources at this particular moment in time. Come back," she said.</p>
