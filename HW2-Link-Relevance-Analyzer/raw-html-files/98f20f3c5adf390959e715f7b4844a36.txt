<p>Marchers filled the streets of downtown Chicago for blocks Saturday night, passing by Trump Tower, and chanting things like "No hate, no fear. Immigrants are welcome here."</p> <p>Kevin Ryan, a U.S. Marine, carried a sign that read "Veterans demand no troops on our street."</p> <p>"It's violation of federal law and it's unconstitutional and it's dangerous," he told NPR. "The military is not trained to police."</p> <p>Similar scenes played out in Washington, D.C. on Saturday, where thousands of protesters marched in the "We Are All D.C." protest. They were condemning federal troops and the deployment of the National Guard in the nation's capital and other cities. </p> <p>DC Metropolitan Police told NPR Saturday evening that it was not aware of any arrests during the protest.</p> <div class="bucketwrap image x-large" id="resg-s1-87516">

<h3 class="edTag"><strong>ChicagoÂ celebrations ongoing</strong></h3> <p>Major events will continue this weekend as scheduled in Chicago, including Taste of Chicago, Printers Row Lit Festival and Sundays on State, but organizers say they'll be monitoring the situation closely, <a href="https://www.wbez.org/things-to-do/2025/09/04/chicago-downtown-weekend-events-ice-national-guard" target="_blank"><u>member station WBEZ</u></a> reported.</p> <p>At least one celebration for Mexican Independence Day, which is on Sept. 16, has been postponed to November because of worries about immigration raids and National Guard deployment, according to <a href="https://www.fox32chicago.com/news/suburban-mexican-independence-day-parade-postponed-over-safety-concerns" target="_blank"><u>Fox 32 Chicago</u></a>.</p> <p>Lugo said her group chose to move forward with their Mexican Independence Day parade this weekend because of the groundswell of support and influx of volunteers they received.</p> <p>"Honestly, it's the support of the community that we have gotten that has kept us going," she said.</p> <p>In the Pilsen neighborhood of Chicago, the Saturday parade began in the early afternoon, but attendees said the normally jam-packed annual event was far sparser in attendance this year â which they attributed to people's concerns over immigration crackdowns. </p> <p>"I have a mixed [immigration status] family, and for them to not be able to go out, all this week they've been relying on me for stuff," said Andrea Soria, a Chicago native who brought her daughter to the parade.</p> <aside aria-label="advertisement" class="ad-wrap third" id="ad-third-wrap">
